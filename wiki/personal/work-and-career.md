# Work and Career Reflections

Thinking about work, identity, career strategy, and what actually matters long-term.
Source: raw/Thoughts 2.md (pts 6, 12, 13, 22, 23, 24, 25, 26) · raw/Thoughts.md (pts 8, 14, 57, 60)

## How to Love the Job (point 6)
The question isn't "how do I push through fatigue" — it's "how do I make genuine engagement the default so fatigue doesn't build." Systems and meaning matter more than discipline here. Connects to point 24 below.

## Keep AI Use at Office Private (point 12)
Don't tell colleagues I'm using AI for my work. Reasons: (1) they will assume I have free time and load me with more. (2) they may adopt my methods and reduce my edge. This is a strategic decision, not dishonesty. See [[near-term-goals]] — the "why the company needs me" answer is the long-game version of this.

## Balancing Workload at a Startup (point 13)
First job, startup context — I know I need to work hard. But I also need time at home for self-improvement and life goals. The challenge: appear eager and initiative-driven while not taking on everything. Can't say no bluntly; need a smarter approach. See [[sequencing-and-systems]] for a structural answer. See [[near-term-goals]] for the prepared-script goal.

## Degree Means Nothing (point 22)
A successful career depends on: (a) communication, (b) constant upskilling. The credential is irrelevant. This reframes where to spend energy.

## Wisdom Timing and Recovery (point 23)
Insight: the knowledge I'm gaining at 23/24 — if I'd had it at 13/14, I'd have succeeded much earlier. But I needed to fail first to learn it. Also: the downfall started at 18, but recovery began fast — enrolled in college at 19, so only one year before the turnaround. That's actually quick. Don't dramatise the lost time.

## Systems Over Motivation (point 24)
Office resentment, goal misalignment, lack of motivation — all of these shrink when reduced to a system. Motivation is unreliable. Design the day so the right behaviour is the default, not the heroic choice. See [[sequencing-and-systems]].

## Personality Audit (point 26)
Identified problems from recent social situations: tongue-tied, shyness, no confidence, not able to say no, tension and anger under stress. These are addressable. Each one needs a specific practice, not just awareness. See [[identity-and-impulse-control]] for the identity layer of this.

## Career After Internship — What's the Plan? (Thoughts 2 pt 25)
I understand the internship builds the CV. But then what? Options: back to college for campus placements at dream companies; or off-campus route for PQC, KPMG, etc. This question needs a structured answer, not just a vague intention. See [[near-term-goals]] for the Google SDE goal which now anchors all of this.

## Performance Visibility — The Introvert Risk (Thoughts 2 pt 26)
Open question: am I performing at the threshold where I won't be laid off? And does staying visible to my boss matter as much as the actual work? The pattern I'm afraid of: do all the hard work, never go to the boss with small updates or questions, get passed over for promotion — become the donkey. All hard work, no gratitude. The fix: small, regular check-ins with the boss. Show up in his short-term memory. Being good at the work is necessary but not sufficient.

## Networking Is a Skill — Study Seetesh (Thoughts pt 8)
Seetesh came across as more of a favourite because of his ability to network. I need to focus on this, especially in the job context. Networking isn't charm for its own sake — it's the visible layer of your value. See [[networking-and-social-habits]] for the habit layer.

## Leadership When Others Don't Deliver (Thoughts pt 14)
Seetesh didn't do good work. I know this will happen in office too. The wrong response: "he won't do it, so neither will I — unfair to me." The question is: how do I, as a workaholic without authority, get the lazy person to contribute without having authority over them? And how do I ensure I get credit for my own work when the dumb ones might take the lead and drop the quality? These are real problems that need real strategies. Come back to this when it becomes live.

## RLLE Lesson — Apply to Office (Thoughts pt 57)
The main problem with RLLE: we didn't spend enough time building connection and trust with the villagers. Survey failed because the relationship wasn't there. The same dynamic applies at work. Relationship first, then the ask. Don't rush to transact before the trust is built.
