## The Foundation
The most basic requirement for any friendship, relationship, or professional network: **be present, be there regularly.** Everything else — conversation quality, charm, shared interests — builds on top of consistency. Without it, nothing sticks. (pt 33)

## Failures of Maintenance — Small Things That Add Up
- Didn't wish friends happy birthday → bad for networking; set reminders (pt 10)
- Missed Aritro Das's New Year wish → read WhatsApp more attentively (pt 20)
- Ignored Tapabrata asking for help by saying "I'm all good" → came across as selfish and rude; this type of dismissal will follow you in a professional context (pt 35)
- Prayas was talking to a stranger — could have joined in, stayed quiet instead → missed an easy networking opportunity (pt 56)

## Taking Initiative — Don't Wait to Be Called
Omkar, Nikhilesh, Sayak: they will not call first. That's just how they are. If I wait for them to reach out, the relationship quietly dies. I have to take initiative. This applies to past networks too — they are a resource you built; maintain them. (pts 54, 44)

## Office Behaviour — Honesty and Back-Biting
- In office: if you can't help, say you can't help. Don't say yes and then disappear — it creates awkwardness and damages trust. The Aneesh/KPMG situation is the cautionary example. Be more like Omkar or Nikhilesh in this. (pt 43a)
- Stop back-biting behind people's backs entirely. (pt 43b)

## Handling Conflict Gracefully
CAS conflict resolution was poor — "I understand your pain" and "don't worry" are not good answers when someone is genuinely upset. Need to learn real conflict handling that: (a) validates what they're feeling, (b) doesn't make promises you can't keep, and (c) keeps you insulated enough to stay functional. Diplomatic language isn't enough — it needs to be backed by genuine problem-solving. (pt 66)

## Being Helpful While Drawing Limits
When someone asks for help, the question is: does this conflict with a personal commitment that day — cooking, studies, own work? If not, help clearly. If yes, say so clearly. The right mix is not random — it's a decision made against a clear picture of what that day requires. Don't make it up on the spot under social pressure. (pt 68) See [[sequencing-and-systems]] for the evening-sequencing system that makes this easier.

## Arpita's Office Visibility Tactic (Thoughts 2 pt 34)
The single most practical networking insight so far: sit near the boss's office and near the centre of the room — not separate in a huddle room. This one physical change meant everyone started coming in and talking naturally. Networking effort dropped by half. Layer on top of that: take lunch with them, join the 5pm break, give a smile, talk kindly, say thank you. The principle is friction reduction — make it easy for people to interact with you by default, and most of the work happens without you having to perform. This directly addresses the performance-visibility risk in [[work-and-career]] (pt 26: the introvert donkey problem).
